Story of Phulkari

Originally done with red flowers, Phulkari is a Punjabi term obtained by combining two words, Phul and Kari, meaning flower and work, respectively. Phulkari is practiced in Amritsar, Jalandhar, Kapurthala, Hoshiarpur, Ludhiana, Ferozepur, Bhattinda, Muktsar, Patiala, and Ambala in Punjab; Hisar in Haryana; and Ganganagar in Rajasthan. Although there are several interpretations of the term, the designs include not only flowers but also cover motifs and geometrical shapes.

 

Etched in the culture of Punjab, Phulkari's origin has a handful of theories. One of them is that an embroidered handkerchief was given to Guru Nanak Dev ji by his sister Bebe Nanaki in a belief to ward off evil. On the other hand, the earliest mention of the craft appears in the famous love story of Heer Ranjha, written by Waris Shah.

Phulkari is an expression of women marveling at the gift of life, narrating festivity and fertility, or just the endeavor of their kind, engulfed in struggle and hardships, to find joy.  

 

The birth of a girl child marks the dawn of a Phulkari dupatta, birthed by her mother and grandmother. As she grows up, it grows with her. Then it accompanies her on her wedding day in her 'Daaj,' which depicts her future life and new relations as a bride. 

For generations, the craft has been passed down to capable hands, and it has only become more substantial, at least in design, if not numeric strength. It embellishes the homes of Punjab to date.

Process of Making Phulkari

 

 

The laborious affair starts with making the ground khadi, homegrown and hand-spun fabric.

Diverse motifs of animals, birds, flowers, or leaves are implicated on the block’s surface which are swiftly plunged and tinted into artificial stainers. The positive surface containing the motif is then imprinted on the fabric, which leaves patterned marks to guide the flow of stitches for the upcoming process. (Some of our dupattas skip this step, depending on the cluster making it and the type of Phulkari)
They are made with high-quality untwisted silk floss called "pat" (dhaga) with bright colors like red, green, golden, yellow, pink, and blue. Intricate and beautiful Phulkari is made with a combination of multiple colors and running the threads in geometric patterns.
The printed fabric is then set in a wooden embroidery frame. A unique homegrown lock technique is used to thread the needle. They are made with high-quality untwisted silk floss called "pat" (dhaga) with bright colors like red, green, golden, yellow, pink, and blue. The needle comes up from the wrong side of the fabric, and the stitching is done on the right side. This technique distinguishes handmade and machine-made embroidery.

The woven khaddar is then dyed in various colors. Maddar is used to give it a deep reddish brown or black shade. Indigo is used to stain it blue.
